The 8 Best Ceiling Fans of 2024, Tested and Reviewed

Summer is in full swing, and you might be looking for new ways to stay cool. Running your air conditioning at all times is costly, so investing in a ceiling fan is a great way to get some extra airflow and cool off in a way that’s both space- and cost-efficient, especially if you install a ceiling fan in your outdoor space. From remote-operated, 3-blade to 5-blade, wooden to metal, the type of ceiling fan you choose largely depends on your preferences, and there are many options on the market. Installing a ceiling fan is a long-term investment in your home, so picking one that gets you the best bang for your buck is important.  

A ceiling fan can add a level of craftsmanship to your space, so choose one that performs well and fits your space visually. “I like compact ceiling fans with a discrete design; those are typically a good choice for smaller spaces,” says Emily Mackie, founder of Inspired Interiors, “These fans are designed to provide efficient air circulation while minimizing the visual impact and space requirements.” 

As we said, when shopping for ceiling fans, you truly are spoiled with choice. Helping you make smart and empowered choices is our priority at PEOPLE Tested, so we’ve spent over 1,200 hours testing different ceiling fans on the market to give you a comprehensive roundup to choose from. Whether you want to keep your bedroom cool or add a ceiling fan to your outdoor space to make these record-breaking hot summer days more enjoyable, we found a ceiling fan for you.

These are the best ceiling fans PEOPLE Tested.

Things to Consider Before Buying a Ceiling Fan

Setup

Installing a ceiling fan isn’t the same as assembling a piece of furniture or regular standing fan. You have to mount it to your ceiling and account for things like electrical connection and wiring — not a concern with other household fans — which can get very treacherous if you aren’t experienced. Looking at a fan’s product page for things like how many parts the fan comes with and what tools you’ll need will give you an adequate picture of what to expect when assembling (and if you’ll have the resources to do so. The Hampton Bay Mena 54 is a great option if you’re looking for a fan that has easy setup; the blades snap right into the center console, which will save you loads of time you would have otherwise spent screwing each blade into place. 

Design

Looking at a fan’s hardware and how it’s made and assembled will tell you how well it works. For example, if the blades are made of a bulky material, they’re more likely to wobble and feel unstable when powered up. Another thing to consider is the ceiling fan's potential location. If you’re going to install it in a small room, picking a fan with fewer blades and a smaller blade span (like the Harber Breeze 44-inch Ceiling Fan) might make more sense visually. If you place it in a larger room, a fan with more blades hanging lower from the ceiling would adequately fill the space. If it will be exposed to the elements like outdoor furniture, you should opt for an outdoor ceiling fan that is treated to be weather- and moisture-resistant or clearly says on the product page that it is built for outdoor use. 

Value

Your budget will obviously be the determining factor in what ceiling fan you choose, but it isn’t necessarily true that you have to break the bank for a good quality product. You can find good deals by monitoring a ceiling fan you like to see when it goes on sale, and combing through product reviews on “budget” ceiling fans to find the hidden gems. A ceiling fan’s value doesn’t all come down to the amount you pay upfront though: Another good rule of thumb is checking whether or not a fan has a warranty, and how long it lasts. This will also help you leverage how much use you will get from it and how far your money will go.

Frequently Asked Questions

  • Is a 3-blade or 5-blade ceiling fan better?

    You may assume that the more blades a fan comes with, the more power it will have. But this is not true! On the contrary, the amount of blades makes little to no difference in a ceiling fan's airflow strength. Three-blade fans oftentimes actually perform better than five-blade fans, because the lighter load on the motor allows it to work more efficiently. The amount of blades on a fan really only makes a difference aesthetically. As we said earlier, if you’re working with a bigger room, more blades might aesthetically make more sense, while three blades may keep a smaller room from looking too cluttered and busy. In that sense, the number of blades just comes down to visual preference.

  • What's the most effective fan blade shape?

    If you want to get into the mathematics of it, a fan’s efficiency is measured by its CFM, which stands for Cubic Feet per Minute. You may see this on the product pages of the ceiling fans you’re perusing. iI’s ultimately meant to quantify how much air is being pushed throughout a space by that fan in one minute. For scale, an “average” fan has a CFM of about 4,000-5,000, while the highest performing fans will have a CFM of 6,000+. A good fan will do a good job of toggling between staying lightweight enough that it will prevent the motor from lagging but also being sturdy enough to churn out lots of air that will keep that CFM high. With this in mind, thin but wide paddle-shaped fan blades do the best job of keeping pressure off the motor and putting out enough air that it manages a room’s temperature consistently in a way you can feel.

  • Can I use an "indoor ceiling fan" outdoors?

    You may have found an “indoor ceiling fan” that would fit perfectly on your patio, or you already have one lying around that you need to use. But according to Mackie, it’s probably best to avoid any mixing and matching. “For longevity in outdoor settings, it's important to choose a material that is rust-resistant and capable of withstanding alternative climates,” she says, “I was recently in the Virgin Islands for a project, and there was a lot of talk of marine-grade material. So marine-grade material would be heavy plastics or any anti-rust metal. These are often recommended for outdoor environments, especially in coastal areas. These materials are designed to withstand exposure to moisture, salt, and other outdoor elements.” In short, you should keep indoor fans inside to get the most use out of your ceiling fan.
